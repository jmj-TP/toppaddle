<p>For players attempting to emulate the explosive forehand of the Chinese National Team, the legendary <a href="/product/rubber/dhs-hurricane-3-neo">DHS Hurricane 3 Neo</a> is usually the first stop. Its tacky topsheet catches the ball differently than any European tensor, allowing for incredibly fast, spinny loops right off the bounce.</p>

<p>But there is a well-known secret: professional Chinese players "boost" their Hurricane rubbers. Boosting involves applying VOC-free oils to the sponge to expand its pores, drastically increasing the speed, catapult effect, and "click" sound of the traditionally dead, hard Chinese sponges.</p>

<h2>The Hassle of Boosting</h2>
<p>If you purchase a commercial sheet of <a href="/product/rubber/dhs-hurricane-3-neo">DHS Hurricane 3 Neo</a> today, it comes "factory-tuned" (a light layer of booster is pre-applied). However, this effect fades after a month or two. Rewriting the sponge requires taking the rubber off your blade, cleaning off the old glue, applying multiple layers of booster oil, waiting 48 hours for the rubber dome to flatten, and re-gluing it.</p>

<p>For amateur and club-level players, this repetitive chemical maintenance is exhausting and inconsistent.</p>

<h2>Modern Hybrid Alternatives to Boosting</h2>
<p>If you want the sticky, heavy topspin of a Chinese rubber without the hassle of a chemistry set, equipment manufacturers have finally caught up. Enter "Hybrids": European-style tensor sponges paired with tacky Chinese-style topsheets.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Tibhar Hybrid K3:</strong> Known for extreme grip, combining a tacky topsheet with a massive 53-degree tensor sponge. It plays like a heavily boosted Chinese rubber straight out of the package.</li>
  <li><strong>Butterfly Dignics 09C:</strong> The current benchmark for hybrid rubbers, offering immense grip and high-end tensor speed without the need for tuning oils.</li>
</ul>
